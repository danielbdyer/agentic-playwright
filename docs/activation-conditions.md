# The Activation Conditions

> Status: Draft — fourth document in the domain modeling series

The domain model (`domain-model.md`) names the 22 primitives — the **semantics**. The upper ontology (`upper-ontology.md`) names the 12 motifs, 12 algebras, and 8 flows — the **syntax**. The design calculus (`design-calculus.md`) names the 16 meaning-preserving transformations — the **equational theory**. This document names the **coherence conditions**: the laws that govern how the algebras compose when the system's combinatorial demand finally loads them.

## What This Document Is

The design calculus achieved its stated goal. All 16 transformations are implemented, wired, and law-tested. The telos described in Part V — one generic FSM, one product fold, one envelope-receipt adjunction, one confidence-provenance lattice — exists in code. The absent abstractions have names. The dualities generate. The free theorems are verified.

But a new observation has become visible. The algebras were designed and tested in isolation. Each satisfies its own laws. The Galois connection verifies its adjunction. The governance lattice verifies its meet. The observation collapse verifies its monoid homomorphism. Individually, they are correct.

The question this document addresses: **do they compose?**

When the governance lattice meets the confidence-provenance Galois connection at the proposal activation boundary — when a `GovernanceVerdict<T, I>` must round-trip through a file-based decision bridge and return as a typed resumption — when the observation-aggregate-compare variant needs context that the base `ObservationCollapse` signature doesn't carry — the system's algebraic structures must interact. The interaction points are where correctness is most at risk, because they are where the laws of one algebra constrain the behavior of another.

This document identifies five specific composition points where the algebras are currently **idle** — structurally present but not load-bearing — and specifies the coherence conditions they must satisfy when the system's growing combinatorial demand activates them. Each section names the idle algebra, traces the production flow that bypasses it, identifies the activation condition that would force it into service, and states the coherence law that must hold.

The framing is deliberate. The issue is not that the algebras are wrong. It is that they have never been *composed under load*. A bridge that has passed every materials test can still fail at a resonance frequency that no individual test excited. The activation conditions are the resonance frequencies of the algebraic architecture.

## Part I: The Governance Lossy Projection

### The idle algebra

The governance lattice (`lib/domain/algebra/lattice.ts`) defines a three-element bounded lattice:

```
blocked ⊑ review-required ⊑ approved
```

with `meet` (infimum, most restrictive) and `join` (supremum, most permissive). The `GovernanceLattice` constant exports `top = 'approved'`, `bottom = 'blocked'`, and the `meet`/`join` operations. The phantom branded types (`Approved<T>`, `ReviewRequired<T>`, `Blocked<T>`) encode governance at the type level. The exhaustive fold `foldGovernance` forces callers to handle all three cases.

The proposal lifecycle FSM (`lib/domain/proposal/lifecycle.ts`) correctly generates all three governance states. When `transitionProposal` receives a `trust-policy-review` event, it produces an activated-but-uncertified result — the `review-required` middle value that the lattice was built to represent.

### The production bypass

At `lib/application/knowledge/activate-proposals.ts:104–109`, the three-valued lattice is projected to two values:

```typescript
const proposalGovernances = proposals.map((p) =>
  isBlocked(p.activation) ? 'blocked' as const : 'approved' as const,
);
const bundleGovernance = proposalGovernances.reduce(
  GovernanceLattice.meet, GovernanceLattice.top,
);
```

The `review-required` state generated by `transitionProposal` is mapped to `'approved'`. The lattice meet then operates on a degenerate two-element sublattice where it reduces to `min()`. The Galois connection between rung and confidence — which maps resolution provenance to a minimum confidence level, and whose `review-required` image is the set of rungs that produce uncertain-but-usable bindings — never composes with this lattice because the intermediate value is discarded before composition can occur.

### Why it's bypassed

The current system operates in dogfood mode, where auto-approval thresholds are permissive. The `review-required` state exists to support production execution profiles where operator sign-off gates activation. In dogfood, the distinction between "approved" and "approved-but-uncertified" is operationally irrelevant — both proceed. The bypass is not a bug; it is a correct optimization for the current execution profile.

### The activation condition

The algebra becomes load-bearing when:

1. **Production execution profiles** gate activation on certification status. A `ci-batch` profile that refuses to execute uncertified knowledge requires the `review-required` state to propagate through the bundle's governance, because the bundle must surface the review obligation to the operator rather than silently proceeding.

2. **The dashboard decision bridge** routes `review-required` proposals to a distinct approval queue. Currently, the `Suspended` verdict in `GovernanceVerdict<T, I>` carries `needs: unknown` — untyped. When the decision bridge must distinguish "this proposal needs trust-policy review" from "this proposal needs operator content review," the `I` parameter must be instantiated with a proper discriminated union, and the file-based `WorkItemDecision` must carry enough structure to round-trip the distinction.

3. **The Galois connection composes with governance** to derive review obligations from provenance. A binding that resolved at rung 7 (live-DOM exploration) has confidence `agent-proposed`. The Galois connection maps this to `review-required` governance. If the confidence-governance composition is honored, the system can *derive* review requirements from resolution provenance rather than relying on explicit trust-policy evaluation — a strictly stronger guarantee.

### The coherence law

When activated, the following must hold:

**Lattice homomorphism**: The projection from `ProposalActivation` to `Governance` must be a lattice homomorphism — it must preserve meet:

```
project(meet(a, b)) = meet(project(a), project(b))
```

The current binary projection satisfies this trivially (it maps to the two-element sublattice, which is closed under meet). But a three-valued projection that preserves `review-required` must also satisfy the law, which constrains how the FSM's activation states map to governance values.

**Galois-governance coherence**: The Galois connection `α : Rung → Confidence` and a new monotone map `β : Confidence → Governance` must compose such that `β ∘ α` is monotone:

```
rung₁ ≤ rung₂  ⟹  β(α(rung₁)) ⊑ β(α(rung₂))
```

Higher rungs (more trusted provenance) must map to governance values at least as permissive as lower rungs. This is the coherence condition that connects resolution provenance to activation governance through the confidence lattice — three algebras composing through two monotone maps.

**Round-trip safety**: A `GovernanceVerdict<T, I>` that suspends with `needs: ReviewRequest` must, when the corresponding `WorkItemDecision` arrives with `status: 'approved'`, produce a verdict equivalent to `Approved<T>` — not a fresh verdict that loses the original value `T`. The decision bridge must close the fiber with the *same typed payload* that was suspended, not a reconstructed approximation.
